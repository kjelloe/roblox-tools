Overview

The MultiAnimation plugin panel has a single compact row (next to Scene name and Export) that contains all camera controls. Below the CONTROLS section, the PROPS IN SCENE area contains the Track Effect button.

Button 1 — 📷 Cam KF  (Capture a Shot) Camera

This is how you record a camera position into the timeline. Each press saves the viewport's position, rotation, and field of view as a keyframe at the current frame number.

How to use it

1
Navigate to the frame where you want the shot. Use the ◄ ► frame-step buttons or type a frame number.
2
Fly the Studio viewport to the exact angle you want — position, rotation, and zoom all matter.
3
Click 📷 Cam KF (or press C). An orange dot appears on the Camera lane in the timeline, and a small gizmo part appears in the scene showing the look direction.
Camera
Three camera keyframes — orange = smooth move between them

Example: Establishing shot + close-up

  1. Go to frame 1 → fly back to show the whole scene → click 📷 Cam KF
  2. Go to frame 30 → fly in close to a character's face → click 📷 Cam KF
  3. Press ▶ Preview — the camera glides smoothly from the wide shot to the close-up over 30 frames.
Tip: You can capture as many keyframes as you like. The camera interpolates (glides) between consecutive keyframes automatically. Add more keyframes for tighter control over the arc of the camera path.
Note: You cannot capture a camera keyframe while Cam:ON preview is active — the viewport is showing the track, so capturing would just re-record the same shot. The plugin will warn and skip. Toggle preview off first, reframe, then capture.

Button 2 — Cam:OFF / Cam:ON  (Preview the Track) Camera

Toggles camera preview mode. When ON, the Studio edit-mode viewport becomes your "virtual camera" — it moves exactly as the exported camera would move in-game.

How to use it

1
After adding some camera keyframes, click Cam:OFF. The button flips to Cam:ON and the viewport snaps to your first camera keyframe.
2
Scrub the timeline or press ▶ Preview. Your viewport follows the camera track — you see exactly what players would see.
3
Click Cam:ON to toggle preview off. The viewport returns to exactly where it was before you turned preview on.

Example: Review your cutscene

  1. Record poses for your rigs across frames 1–90.
  2. Add camera keyframes at frames 1, 30, 60, and 90.
  3. Click Cam:OFF to enable preview.
  4. Press ▶ Preview — watch the whole cutscene play back, with both rig animation and camera movement, without entering play mode.
  5. Click Cam:ON to return to free-look mode and adjust any shots you don't like.
Tip: While in preview mode you can still scrub the timeline with the ◄ ► buttons or drag the scrubber to step through the track frame-by-frame and judge every angle.

Button 3 — KF:— / KF:move / KF:cut  (Move or Hard Cut) Camera

Each camera keyframe has a mode: either move (smooth interpolation from the previous shot) or cut (the previous shot freezes, then the camera jumps hard at this exact frame).

  • Orange dot = move — camera glides to this shot
  • Red dot = cut — camera jumps here instantly

The button shows KF:— when there is no keyframe at the current frame, KF:move or KF:cut when there is.

How to toggle a keyframe's mode

1
Navigate to the frame that has the keyframe you want to change (the button shows KF:move or KF:cut).
2
Click the KF:move / KF:cut button to flip the mode. The dot on the timeline changes colour instantly.
Camera
Frame 1 (orange=move) → Frame 50 (red=cut, instant jump) → Frame 90 (orange=move)

Example: Multi-angle action sequence

You want a wide shot, then a hard cut to a different angle, then a slow pull-back:

  1. Frame 1 → wide establishing shot → 📷 Cam KF → leave as KF:move
  2. Frame 30 → close-up on character A → 📷 Cam KF → click KF:move to flip it to KF:cut (red dot)
  3. Frame 60 → character B reacts → 📷 Cam KF → flip to KF:cut
  4. Frame 90 → pull back to wide → 📷 Cam KF → leave as KF:move

Result: wide shot holds until frame 30, hard cut to close-up of A, hard cut to B reacting, then the camera smoothly glides back to wide.

Tip: The first keyframe (frame 1) is almost always a move — it just sets the starting position. Use cut for dramatic angle changes and move for smooth transitions.

Interaction 4 — Delete a Camera Shot Camera

Right-click any orange or red dot on the Camera lane to delete that keyframe. The gizmo part in the scene disappears at the same time.

How to delete

1
Find the dot on the Camera lane in the timeline.
2
Right-click the dot. It disappears immediately along with its viewport gizmo.
Undo: Camera keyframe deletion is not currently undoable with Ctrl+Z. Re-capture the shot if you accidentally delete it.

Example: Remove a redundant shot

You placed keyframes at frames 1, 20, 40, and 60 but the frame-20 shot is too similar to frame-1. Right-click the dot at frame 20 to remove it. The camera now glides from frame 1 directly to frame 40.

Interaction 5 — Reposition a Shot via Gizmo Camera

Every camera keyframe has a small gizmo part in the scene (a box with a stud pointing in the look direction). You can use the Studio move and rotate tools directly on the gizmo to re-aim the shot without recapturing.

How to reposition

1
Click a gizmo in the viewport. The timeline jumps to that keyframe's frame.
2
Use the Studio Move tool (or Rotate) to drag the gizmo to the new camera position and direction. The keyframe updates in real time.
3
Enable Cam:OFF → Cam:ON and scrub to verify the new angle.
Tip: Gizmos are never saved with the place file and vanish when the plugin unloads. They are a preview aid only — the actual keyframe data lives in the session.

Example: Fine-tune a close-up

Your close-up at frame 45 is slightly off-axis. Click the gizmo for frame 45, rotate it a few degrees left, and the keyframe updates. Toggle preview on and play back to confirm the new angle.

Interaction 6 — Add a Shot by Double-clicking the Lane Camera

Double-clicking anywhere on the Camera track lane is a shortcut that combines two steps: it jumps the timeline to that frame and immediately captures the current viewport as a camera keyframe.

How to use it

1
Make sure your Studio viewport is already framed the way you want.
2
Double-click a blank spot on the Camera lane at the target frame. A dot appears immediately.
Tip: This is faster than: navigate frame → fly camera → click button. Use it when you want to quickly rough-in a series of shots by framing each angle and double-clicking the corresponding frame position.

Full Cutscene Workflow — End to End Camera

Here is a complete example: a 5-second cutscene (120 frames at 24 fps) where two characters talk and the camera cuts between them.

Step 1 — Record character animation

  1. Open MultiAnimation, confirm Rig1 and Rig2 are detected in the RIGS section.
  2. Pose Rig1 for each key moment, pressing K (or + Add Keyframe) to record. Do the same for Rig2.
  3. Press ▶ Preview to check the animation looks right.

Step 2 — Add camera keyframes

1
Go to frame 1. Position the viewport for an establishing wide shot showing both characters. Press C to capture.
2
Go to frame 25. Zoom in on Rig1's face. Press C. Then click KF:move to make it a KF:cut.
3
Go to frame 55. Pivot to Rig2. Press C. Flip to KF:cut.
4
Go to frame 90. Wide shot again. Press C. Leave as KF:move (smooth pull-back).
5
Go to frame 120. Slightly higher angle. Press C. Leave as KF:move.
Rig1
Rig2
Camera
fr1(wide·move) → fr25(Rig1 close·cut) → fr55(Rig2 cut) → fr90(wide·move) → fr120(high·move)

Step 3 — Preview and adjust

  1. Click Cam:OFF to enter preview mode.
  2. Press ▶ Preview and watch the full cutscene.
  3. To adjust a shot: toggle preview off, click the gizmo for that shot, re-aim with the Move/Rotate tools, preview again.
  4. To delete a bad shot: right-click its dot and recapture.

Step 4 — Export and play in-game

-- In a Script inside ServerScriptService:
local Cutscene = require(game.ServerStorage.MultiAnimationData.CutsceneServer)
Cutscene.play("Scene_001", {
    Rig1 = workspace.FIGURES.Rig1,
    Rig2 = workspace.FIGURES.Rig2,
})

This fires the cutscene for all connected players simultaneously. Their cameras are driven by the CameraTrack module that was exported alongside the scene.

Animation only (no cutscene camera): If you just want the rig animation without the camera track, use MultiAnimPlayer.play() instead. The camera track is only fired when you call CutsceneServer.play().

The Track Effect Button Effect Track

The Track Effect button (in the PROPS IN SCENE section, below the FX: label) lets you place one-shot events — particle bursts, sound cues, light flashes — at specific frames on the timeline. When the exported animation plays back, these events fire at exactly the right moment, no manual scripting required.

Supported effect types

TypeRoblox classAvailable actions
Particle burst ParticleEmitter emit (burst of 15 particles) · on (enable continuous) · off (disable)
Sound cue Sound play · stop
Toggle PointLight, SpotLight,
SurfaceLight, Beam,
Trail, Highlight		 on · off

How tracking works

You don't need to select the effect instance itself. You can click any part that contains the effect — the plugin walks its descendants to find the first compatible instance automatically.

UI elementWhat it is
Purple chip The tracked effect. Shows its name and current default action. Click to cycle the action; click × to untrack.
Purple lane The effect's timeline lane. Double-click to place an event; right-click an event dot to delete it.

Cycling the default action

The default action is what will be recorded when you double-click the purple lane to place an event. Click the purple chip to cycle through the available actions for that effect type:

  • ParticleEmitter: emit → on → off → emit …
  • Sound: play → stop → play …
  • Lights / Beam / Trail: on → off → on …

Cycling the default action does not change events already placed on the lane — only new events pick up the new default.

Effect Track Workflows — Examples Effect Track

Example A — Particle burst on impact

You have a magic attack animation. At frame 24 the fist makes contact. You want a spark burst to fire at exactly that moment.

1
In the workspace, select the BasePart that contains your SparkEmitter (ParticleEmitter). Click Track Effect. A purple chip labelled SparkEmitter appears with action emit.
2
Navigate to frame 24.
3
Double-click the purple lane at frame 24. A purple dot appears — the event is recorded as emit (×15).
4
Export the scene. At playback, when the animation reaches frame 24 the emitter fires a burst of 15 particles.

SparkEmitter

Example B — Sound cue + light flash

An explosion at frame 40: you want a BoomSound to play and a PointLight to flash on then off.

1
Select the part containing BoomSound → click Track Effect. Chip shows BoomSound · play.
2
Select the part containing the PointLight → click Track Effect. Chip shows PointLight · on.
3
Go to frame 40. Double-click the BoomSound lane → records play. Double-click the PointLight lane → records on.
4
Go to frame 46. Click the PointLight chip to cycle action to off. Double-click the PointLight lane → records off. Click the chip again to cycle back to on for future events.
5
Export. At playback: frame 40 → boom plays + light turns on; frame 46 → light turns off. The flash lasts exactly 6 frames.

Example C — Continuous particle trail during a charge-up

A character charges a power-up from frame 10 to frame 50. You want the AuraEmitter on during the charge and off when it ends.

1
Track the part containing AuraEmitter. Chip shows AuraEmitter · emit.
2
Click the chip once: action cycles to on.
3
Go to frame 10 → double-click the lane → records on.
4
Click the chip again: action cycles to off.
5
Go to frame 50 → double-click the lane → records off.
6
Export. At playback the aura effect runs continuously from frame 10 to frame 50.
Tip: Effect events are one-shot — there is no interpolation or "duration" concept. To turn something on for a period, you need two events: an on event at the start frame and an off event at the end frame.

Example D — Multiple effects synchronized

A landing impact at frame 60: dust particles, a thud sound, and a shockwave highlight all trigger simultaneously.

  1. Track DustEmitter → action emit.
  2. Track ThudSound → action play.
  3. Track ShockwaveHighlight → action on.
  4. Go to frame 60 → double-click each of the three purple lanes.
  5. Track ShockwaveHighlight chip → cycle to off → go to frame 66 → double-click its lane.
  6. Export → all three fire together on frame 60; the highlight clears at frame 66.

Removing a wrongly-tracked effect

Click × on the purple chip. The lane and all its events are removed from the UI. The session data (events) is preserved — if you re-track the same instance, the events reappear.

To also clear the saved events, start a new session (New button with confirmation), or simply don't export — only effects with at least one event are included in the export.
